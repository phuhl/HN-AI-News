        content_bullets:
          - "The system uses Ebbinghaus forgetting curves (a 19th-century memory research framework): stored memories lose retrieval weight over time unless accessed, with consolidation intervals resetting decay."
          - "52% recall at 30 days compares favorably to the ~25% baseline for unreviewed human memories, though benchmark validity depends on how 'recall' is measured for an agent."
          - "Implementation uses a vector database with decaying cosine-similarity weights; a background process periodically re-embeds items based on access frequency and time since last retrieval."
          - "Frequently accessed or heavily linked memories decay more slowly, mimicking emotional and contextual salience — important context persists without requiring the agent to explicitly flag it."
        discussion_bullets:
          - "Commenters questioned whether biological decay is the right model at all — human forgetting is an evolutionary accident, not a feature; importance-based pruning may be more principled."
          - "Technical discussion focused on scaling: decaying similarity scores rather than removing embeddings means the vector index grows indefinitely, raising latency concerns for large long-running agents."
          - "One commenter shared a competing approach using LLM-generated importance scores at ingest rather than time-based decay, sparking discussion about which handles novel or surprising information better."
