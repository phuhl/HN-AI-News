    link: https://blog.lyc8503.net/en/post/llm-classifier/
    domain: blog.lyc8503.net
    summary: Classical ML detects LLM-generated text with ~85% single-sentence accuracy
      and sub-0.01% false positives — showing today's AI output carries detectable
      statistical fingerprints even after light post-processing.
    points: 0
    hn_url: https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48936880
    comments: 0
    time: 'Jul 16, 09:30 UTC'
    content_bullets:
    - 'Feature extraction targets statistical signatures unique to LLM output: token-level
      log-probabilities, perplexity scores, and burstiness — LLMs produce text with
      unusually uniform entropy compared to humans, who vary unpredictably in word
      choice and rhythm.'
    - A lightweight classical classifier (logistic regression or random forest) is
      trained on those features, requiring no GPU and producing interpretable decision
      boundaries — a deliberate contrast to heavyweight neural detection approaches.
    - 'Single-sentence detection accuracy reaches ~85% on the test set; at a 70% confidence
      threshold the false positive rate drops below 0.01%, meaning fewer than 1 in
      10,000 human-written sentences are incorrectly flagged.'
    - The detector was validated against LLM-generated web fiction, a domain where
      output is polished and often human-edited, suggesting the statistical signature
      survives light post-processing.
    - 'Key limitation: detection relies on access to a reference LLM to compute perplexity
      and log-probabilities, so the method is model-family-dependent and may degrade
      when the generating model differs significantly from the scoring model; adversarial
      paraphrasing could also reduce the statistical gap.'
    discussion_bullets:
    - HN commenters were skeptical of the 85% sentence-level headline, noting accuracy
      likely improves substantially at paragraph or document level — but also that
      real-world use cases (academic integrity) often involve short passages, making
      sentence-level performance the operationally relevant number.
    - A recurring thread debated whether future LLMs will adapt — models trained with
      human-preference feedback may naturally learn to mimic human perplexity distributions,
      rendering perplexity-based features obsolete; the detector is a snapshot of
      current model behavior, not a durable signal.
    - The sub-0.01% false positive rate drew both praise (the only metric that matters
      when falsely accusing a student) and concern — it was measured on one corpus and
      may not hold across languages, styles, or non-fiction where human text can be
      low-perplexity.
