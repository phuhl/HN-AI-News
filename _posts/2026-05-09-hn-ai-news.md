  - title: A polynomial autoencoder beats PCA on transformer embeddings
    link: https://ivanpleshkov.dev/blog/polynomial-autoencoder/
    domain: ivanpleshkov.dev
    summary: A 150-line numpy trick beats PCA at compressing transformer embeddings by using a polynomial decoder to recover nonlinear structure that linear methods miss
    points: 99
    hn_url: https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48021023
    comments: 28
    time: May 8, 05:45 UTC
    content_bullets:
    - The method pairs a standard PCA encoder with a quadratic polynomial decoder solved via Ridge regression — no neural networks, no SGD, just one closed-form matrix operation.
    - On the FiQA retrieval benchmark with mxbai-embed-large-v1, Poly-AE at 256d scores NDCG@10 of 0.4441 vs PCA's 0.4168 — recovering 2.73 percentage points of the 3.58 p.p. lost by PCA (Principal Component Analysis, the standard linear dimensionality reduction baseline).
    - Gains across four embedding models range from +0.03 to +4.4 percentage points at 128 dimensions; improvement scales with embedding anisotropy — the more 'cone-like' the embedding geometry, the bigger the win.
    - The quadratic lift dimension grows as M=(d+1)(d+2)/2, so d=256 yields M=33,153 features; cubic scaling makes d>256 computationally prohibitive, and the method needs corpus statistics upfront, ruling out streaming or multi-tenant use.
    - MIT-licensed implementation is ~150 lines of numpy, reproducible on consumer hardware in 30–40 minutes, available at github.com/IvanPleshkov/poly-autoencoder.
    discussion_bullets:
    - Several commenters noted this is essentially kernel PCA with a polynomial kernel — the author's contribution is making it practical at transformer-embedding scale efficiently and in closed form.
    - The author confirmed the method is fully unsupervised; compression is driven by minimizing reconstruction error, with the polynomial decoder learning to recover nonlinear variance that linear PCA discards.
    - Skeptics asked for downstream retrieval benchmark results and testing across more architectures before drawing broad conclusions; one commenter drew parallels to KV-cache quantization work that exploits the same anisotropy observation.
