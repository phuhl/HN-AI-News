    content_bullets:
    - AI matches human accuracy on CAPTCHA tasks, but the underlying process is detectably different — sequential click patterns, direction changes, and overselection behaviors reveal machine origins.
    - 'Researchers propose a ''Process Turing Test'': instead of matching outputs, the bar is producing a process indistinguishable from human cognition — a much harder target.'
    - They built CogCAPTCHA30, combining standard image CAPTCHAs with 29 cognitive-psychology tasks spanning decision-making, memory, perception, and reasoning.
    - Counterintuitively, frontier models (Claude, GPT, Gemini) are *less* human-like than smaller models; Centaur, fine-tuned on 10M+ human choices, came closest to passing.
    - When AI had full knowledge of the discriminator's features, the gap closed — but the advantage vanished under cross-task generalization, suggesting the method stays robust when models lack complete
      evaluation info.
    discussion_bullets:
    - 'HN commenters noted the real differentiator is behavioral biometrics, not visual solving: humans hesitate at semantic boundaries while AI hesitates uniformly, making random-delay spoofing insufficient.'
    - The arms-race framing was contested — one commenter argued CAPTCHAs only need to be expensive enough to deter mass automation, so even 85% detection accuracy is practically useful.
    - Playwright-driven agents (real browser automation) are harder to detect than raw API calls but still betray themselves through distinctive focus events and scroll behavior, per the researchers.
